# The Kinnaur–Spiti Road, Mile by Mile

Nine days, one road, and a valley that changes character every twenty kilometres — from apple orchards in Kinnaur to lunar fossil beds in Langza. A practical account of where to stop, where to sleep, and where to just sit quietly.

## The Shimla to Kalpa Stretch

The journey starts in Shimla, but the real road begins only after you cross Narkanda and drop down to the Sutlej river. The gorge is deep, the road carved straight into the rock face. As you climb towards Kalpa, the green pine forests give way to the spectacular sight of the Kinner Kailash range, its snow-capped peaks changing colors in the evening sun.

Kalpa is your first acclimatization stop at 2,960 metres. Walk through the old village, look at the traditional wooden temples, and get used to the thinner air.

---

## Crossing the Border into Spiti

After Kalpa, the road follows the Sutlej until Khab, the dramatic confluence of the Sutlej and Spiti rivers. From here, you begin the steep climb into the Spiti valley. The landscape changes instantly — the trees vanish, replaced by yellow mud cliffs and barren slopes.

*   **Nako**: A high-altitude village with a sacred lake and ancient monastery.
*   **Tabo**: Home to a 1,000-year-old monastery with incredible clay statues and murals.
*   **Dhankar**: A fort and monastery perched precariously on a cliff overlooking the river confluence.

---

## The High Valleys of Spiti

From Kaza, the sub-divisional headquarters, you can explore the high-altitude villages:

1.  **Kee Monastery**: The iconic fortress-like monastery that defines Spiti.
2.  **Kibber**: One of the highest inhabited villages in the world.
3.  **Langza**: The valley of fossils, where you can find marine fossils dating back millions of years to the Tethys Sea.

The journey ends as you cross the Kunzum Pass (4,590 metres) and drop down to the Lahaul valley, but the silence of Spiti stays with you long after.
