# Walking on a Frozen River: Six Days on the Chadar

There is no road to Zanskar in winter. There is only the river, frozen hard enough to walk on, and the eleven of us who decided that was reason enough to go. This is what nobody tells you about the Chadar before you do it.

## The First Step on Ice

The first step you take on the frozen Zanskar river is not one of triumph. It is one of absolute, concentrated fear. Beneath your boots, the ice is a living, changing creature. In some places, it is thick and white, solid as concrete. In others, it is thin, a glassy sheet through which you can see the emerald-green water rushing at a speed that would sweep you under in seconds.

We started at Chilling, the road-end where the metal tarmac simply slides into the rocky rubble. The temperature was already -12°C. 

Here is what you learn on day one: **do not walk normally**. You must slide. The Zanskari guides call it the "penguin walk." You keep your feet flat, close to the ice, and shuffle your weight from side to side. It feels ridiculous. But within an hour, when you see a fellow trekker go sprawling onto their back with a resounding *crack*, you stop laughing and start penguin-walking with absolute devotion.

---

## The Cold That Linger

In Zanskar, cold is not just a temperature; it is a physical weight. During the day, under the bright mountain sun, you are comfortable in your layers as long as you are moving. But once the sun drops behind the high canyon walls—which happens around 3:00 PM—the temperature plunges.

At night, inside the caves or tents, the mercury hits -25°C. 

*   Your water bottles freeze solid inside your sleeping bag.
*   Your camera batteries drain in minutes.
*   The condensation from your breath freezes into a fine frost on the inner tent lining, which snows down on your face if you move too suddenly.

And yet, there is a strange sukoon (peace) in this frozen wilderness. There are no mobile networks. No emails. There is only the sound of the cracking ice, the roaring river, and the absolute silence of the stars above the high Zanskar canyon walls.

---

## Why We Go

By day six, our hands were chapped, our knees ached from the constant tension of staying upright, and our noses were frostbitten. But as we climbed the final moraine back to Chilling, we realized we had walked through one of the most extraordinary landscapes on earth.

We had seen frozen waterfalls hanging like giant pipe organs from cliffs, slept in caves where Zanskari traders have slept for centuries, and looked up at stars so bright they cast shadows on the white ice.

It is not a trip for everyone. But for those who want to experience the absolute silence of the high Himalayas, the Chadar is the only road.
