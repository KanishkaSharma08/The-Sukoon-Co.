# What 5am Looks Like at India's Darkest Sky Reserve

Hanle sits at 4,500 metres with almost no light pollution for hundreds of kilometres. We went to watch the stars and stayed up arguing about whether the Milky Way looks better through a telescope or with nothing in between you and it.

## The Hanle Plateau

We arrived in Hanle in the late afternoon. The landscape here is stark, even by Ladakh's standards. It is a vast, dry plain ringed by dry, brown hills. At the top of one of these hills stands the Indian Astronomical Observatory, its silver dome glinting in the cold sunlight.

Hanle is one of the highest observatories in the world. The air is thin, dry, and cold. There is less water vapor here than in almost any other inhabited valley in India, which is exactly why the astronomers chose it. Less water vapor means less optical distortion. The sky here is clear almost 270 nights a year.

---

## The Stars Arrive

At 8:00 PM, the sky was completely dark. 

For someone who grew up in the city, the night sky in Hanle is unrecognizable. It is not a black dome with a few scattering dots. It is a crowded, glowing highway of light. The Milky Way was not a faint cloud; it was a bright, textured band of dust and light that looked close enough to touch.

By 2:00 AM, the temperature had dropped to -15°C. We stood outside our homestay wrapped in woollen blankets. 

One of our guides pointed out the constellations:
1.  **Orion's Belt** was rising high in the east.
2.  **Ursa Major** hovered above the northern ridge.
3.  **Mars** burned with a bright, steady orange light in the center of the sky.

---

## 5:00 AM Silence

By 5:00 AM, the stars were beginning to fade, replaced by a deep indigo light rising behind the eastern peaks. The valley was silent. There was no wind, no sound of traffic, no birds. 

Standing in the freezing cold, looking out over the plain, you realize how rare absolute silence has become. In Hanle, the silence is as vast as the sky. It is a place that demands you stop, look up, and be quiet.
